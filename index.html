Rushmore
Wes Anderson 1998

“What’d you think, Mr. Littlejeans?”

Rushmore has always been my go-to answer for my favorite movie. It didn’t always feel true, but it was close enough, and it’s a sort of interesting but uncontroversial answer. I hadn’t seen it in years and years, though, and was a bit nervous to revisit it. It meant a lot to me growing up, and I was worried it might not hold up.

And boy, did it mean a lot to me as a 16-year-old when it came out. It not only opened up a wider world of movies to me, but a wider world in general. It hit me right in my developing sense of humor, and appealed to the instant nostalgia for the present you only really feel as a teenager. And I mean, it gave me a bunch of new bands to explore. It helped develop my aesthetic sense. All of a sudden I knew about typefaces. It gave me a new definition of what cool could be. I had magazine clippings about this movie hanging on my bedroom wall.

Anyway, it holds up and I still love it. Still makes me LOL. It’s impossible for me to have an opinion about it that isn’t colored by my existing relationship with it, but I don’t really want to watch it without that existing relationship. That’s kind of the point.

Do the Right Thing

Watching this (and all of Spike Lee’s movies) this year, with Trump in the White House and George Floyd and so many others dead, was one of the more eye-opening cultural experiences of my life. The fact that Spike has been talking about this stuff in his movies for decades and I’m just now starting to get it is a real sign of my white privilege.

On top of all that, even with the darkness and heavy themes floating around the edges, this is still somehow a joyful, fun movie experience.

Malcolm X
Spike Lee 1992

Spike Lee is not an indulgent filmmaker, at least when it comes to length. Most of his movies come in at two hours and change, occasionally even less. When he stretches out a bit, like with this movie and something like When the Levees Broke, you know he has a lot to say. And there are few people I’d rather listen to talk about something they’re passionate about than Spike Lee.

And at over three hours, it still doesn’t feel like there is a wasted moment (OK, maybe some of the early bits). That’s a testament not only to the story being told, but also to the way Spike is telling it. Do the Right Thing is his masterpiece, but this might be his most confident filmmaking.

It almost feels like a cliche, but I have to say it: this is the role Denzel Washington was born to play.

Might be my favorite use of Spike’s famous double dolly shot.

Mishima: A Life in Four Chapters
Paul Schrader 1985

I like a lot of Paul Schrader's movies, maybe most of them. This is the one that really changed how I think about him, though. It's just oozing with creativity, both in its structure and style. The decision to interweave the biographical episodes from Yukio Mishima's life with staged scenes from his novels allows the movie to paint with a full spectrum of human emotions that wouldn't have been possible with only one or the other.

And the score. Maybe the thing I was most grateful to have been introduced to by a movie this year was this score, by Philip Glass.

Blade Runner
Ridley Scott 1982

This is right on the line between four-and-a-half and five stars for me. It’s not without flaws, although I think a lot of the flaws people mention are just intentional noir elements that modern viewers have trouble with, like the slow-ish, moody pace and the flatness of some of the characters. But in the end it gets the full five stars, because the visuals and set design and costume design and makeup and lighting and cinematography are so transcendently good. Almost oppressively good.

Speaking of oppressive, I love the close-ups and claustrophobic framing in this movie. We’re always either right up in someone’s eerily lit face or across the street from them, with people and cars dashing across our vision. I’ve never seen this in a theater, and I’d really like to.

This is just such a cool movie. Maybe the coolest movie. It’s almost a cross between Alien and A Clockwork Orange, stylistically, and that's not a combination I’d kick out of bed for eating noodles.

Ridley doesn’t really take swings like this anymore. I’d love to know what effect the failure of his next movie, Legend, had on his filmmaking. He seemed to want to take far fewer risks as his career went on.

eXistenZ
David Cronenberg 1999

“Death to Realism!”

I’ve been watching all of David Cronenberg’s movies in order, and I’d not particularly been looking forward to this one. I didn’t know anything about it, but, I mean...the title. It certainly didn’t seem like a movie that would age well.

But it turns out that this is the best movie about video games (the only good movie about video games?) ever made. I mean, is David Cronenberg a gamer? Because this movie was made when I was playing my Dreamcast and it’s still the best representation in a movie of what playing a video game is like. It honestly barely feels dated. That said, I still cringed whenever anyone said “Eggsssistaahhhnz.”

I do need to note that I suspect I wouldn’t have liked this nearly as much if I hadn’t spent the past few weeks watching Cronenberg movies. I suspect the tone, had I just walked into a theater in 1999, would have been a bit jarring.

Why were people so into reality blurring movies like this in the late-90s?

Matchstick Men
Ridley Scott 2003

When I lived in Boston I used to get lunch at the McDonald’s in South Station every day. I started going because I loved the Chicken Selects they had for a while, but then they got rid of them and I kept going anyway. It’s not something I’m proud of.

There was always a fair share of shady characters hanging out in the upstairs dining area of the South Station food court, but you got used to it and stopped thinking about it after a while. One day I was eating my bland hamburger and a guy behind me in a scuffed-up black leather jacket started talking to me. At first I was just nodding along politely, hoping he’d leave me alone and let me get back to my podcast.

But then something happened and all of a sudden this guy was reeling me in. I mean, you could tell by looking at him that there was no way he worked in the Celtics’ front office. But he whipped out a beat-up schedule he got at Dunkin’ Donuts and there I was handing him $20 and pointing to an upcoming game against the Bulls, and off he went to make a call and get me my promised courtside seats. I mean, even as it was happening I knew he was getting one over on me. But it worked.

This movie worked on me the same way the first time I saw it. And it still works.

Time

Let me start by saying that I really liked this and it’s the sort of documentary everyone should watch. Let me also say that I’d heard so many effusive reviews of this before seeing it that I had what were probably unfairly high expectations.

But it didn’t totally grab me in the gut in the way I was hoping it would, whether that’s on me or it. I think part of the problem is the way the story was told: in black and white, alternating between old and new footage, no narration, no text on screen, no real introduction to all of the people involved or full backstory of the events. It perhaps added to the mood, but the timeline became a bit confusing and I was left with a lot of questions about certain things. I came away admiring and feeling compassion for many of the people in this documentary, but I didn’t feel like I really knew them. The final third or so of the movie is undeniably powerful, though.

There is quite a list developing over the past few years of movies depicting the hideousness and racism of the American criminal justice system, and this definitely deserves a place near the top of it.
